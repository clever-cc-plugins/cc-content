# Press Release Format Guidelines

Format-scope rules for the `cc-content-press-release` skill. These rules are language-
and industry-neutral: they describe **structure, calibration, and persuasion architecture**,
not voice or vocabulary. Voice comes from the loaded brand voice context; output
language comes from the loaded output language context. All word counts in this file
are **English-equivalent units** — translate proportionally for other languages (German
typically +10–20%, Romance languages roughly equivalent, CJK languages count characters
instead, where ~1.6 CJK characters ≈ 1 English word).

The guidelines are organized in three layers:

- **Layer 1 — Universal best practices.** Apply to every press release.
- **Layer 2 — Audience-specific variations.** B2B/B2C divergence + persuasion stack.
- **Layer 3 — Goal and distribution-channel variations.** Three distinct 2026 functions.

A run-time **quality checklist** at the end summarizes the gates every draft must pass.

## Layer 1 — Universal Best Practices

These apply to every press release regardless of audience, goal, or distribution channel.

### Structure: The inverted pyramid

Press releases follow the **inverted pyramid** — place the most important information first,
supporting details in descending order of importance. This structure ensures the core message
survives even if an editor trims from the bottom. [eReleases, AP Style for Press Releases]

A defensible default structure has **five functional zones**, each with one job:

1. **Headline (title line).** Headline-style title, title case, under 100 characters
   (this length attracts the most views [eReleases, ibid.]). Do NOT include ending
   punctuation unless a question mark or exclamation point is grammatically necessary
   (in practice, use neither). Examples: "SaaS Platform Cuts Customer Onboarding Time
   by 60% with AI-Powered Setup Wizard" (good length and specificity) rather than
   "Company Announces New Product" (generic and too short).

2. **Dateline.** Place the release location at the very start of the first paragraph.
   Use city name in ALL CAPS followed by a long dash with a single space on each side,
   then the body text begins: `CITY — Body text begins here`. Add the state abbreviation
   if the city is not on AP's standalone-city list (e.g., `PORTLAND, Ore. —`, not
   `PORTLAND —`). [eReleases, ibid.]

3. **Lead paragraph.** The opening paragraph must answer the 5 Ws (who, what, when,
   where, why) within 50–75 words. [eReleases, ibid.] This is the hook — it must
   state the announcement clearly and specifically. Avoid generic language like
   "exciting news" or "thrilled to share"; instead, state what happened and why
   it matters. Example: "Acme Inc., a leading SaaS platform for customer success teams,
   today announced a 60% reduction in onboarding time through its new AI-powered setup
   wizard, available immediately to all customers at no additional cost." (specificity,
   numbers, and concrete benefit in one sentence.)

4. **Body paragraphs with supporting detail.** Expand on the announcement with context,
   features, benefits, and business impact. Use descriptive paragraph headers where
   clarity benefits from sectioning. Keep paragraphs to 1–3 sentences each. Front-load
   the most important claim in each paragraph.

5. **Boilerplate paragraph + media contact.** Placed at the very end, just before
   contact information. Keep the boilerplate to 100 words or less (under 50 is better);
   written in third person (not first, which reads as opinionated, or second, which
   reads as advertising copy). It should answer: whom the company helps, what it makes/does,
   and where it's located. [Wylie Comm, Press Release Boilerplate Best Practices]
   Follow with full contact name, title, phone, and email.

### Length and word count

Ideal range: **300–500 words**, with approximately **400 words as the most-recommended
sweet spot** (about one single-spaced page) — long enough to tell the story, short
enough that a journalist scanning 50–100 pitches a day will actually read it.
[PBJ Stories / PressCable, Ideal Press Release Length 2026]

Key threshold: **73% of journalists prefer press releases under 500 words**; releases
over 600 words signal a lack of understanding of media needs. **400–600-word releases
get significantly higher pickup rates than longer ones.** [PressCable, ibid.]

### AP style conventions

Follow these conventions for numerals, dates, and titles:

- **Numerals:** spell out numbers one through nine; use figures for 10+. Always use
  digits for percentages (60%, not sixty percent) and money ($2.5 million, $15K).
- **Dates:** abbreviate months with 6+ letters when paired with a specific date
  (Jan. 12, 2026), spell out shorter months (May 5, 2026). Use the four-digit year.
- **Titles:** capitalize a title when it appears before a name ("CEO Jane Smith");
  lowercase a title when it appears after a name ("Jane Smith, chief executive officer").
  [eReleases, ibid.]

### Quotes: specificity and sourcing

Include **one or two quotes from key spokespeople**. Quotes must add genuine insight —
avoid fluff ("we're thrilled to share…"). Instead, quotes should state something
specific and new that does not restate the lead. Where possible, source quotes from
multiple stakeholders (a third party or customer, not only the company) to add
perspective and authenticity. [Wylie Comm / Content Grip, Press Release Quote Guide]

Attribution format:

- **First reference:** full name + title (e.g., "Jane Smith, CEO of Acme Inc.")
- **Subsequent references:** last name only (e.g., "Smith noted…")

### What separates high from low performers

Clarity, specificity, and third-party credibility are the core differentiators:

- **Specific claims over generic ones.** "Reduces customer onboarding time by 60%"
  beats "significantly improves efficiency."
- **Quotable insights over enthusiasm.** Quotes that state a unique perspective,
  business outcome, or customer benefit outperform generic praise.
- **Third-party validation.** Customer or industry analyst quotes carry more weight
  than internal quotes alone.
- **Inverted pyramid discipline.** Journalists and AI systems that excerpt press
  releases often use the first 1–2 paragraphs. If those paragraphs stand alone as
  a complete announcement, the release works even if the bottom half is edited away.

## Layer 2 — Audience-Specific Variations

### B2B vs. B2C Tone and Approach

| Dimension                     | B2B                                                                   | B2C                                                        |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Tone**                      | Data-driven, formal, business-efficiency-focused                      | Informal, personal benefit, lifestyle-focused              |
| **Language**                  | Industry-specific terminology; assume professional understanding      | Relatable, conversational; avoid jargon                    |
| **Evidence/proof type**       | ROI, efficiency metrics, case-study data, analyst quotes              | Customer testimonials, lifestyle benefit, emotional appeal |
| **Decision-maker psychology** | Business efficiency, cost savings, scalability, competitive advantage | Convenience, lifestyle, status, personal benefit           |
| **Distribution context**      | Trade publications, niche industry media, longer editorial lead times | Consumer media, lifestyle outlets, social distribution     |
| **Relationship goal**         | Build credibility with decision-makers through expertise              | Build emotional connection through storytelling            |

**B2B nuances:** niche audiences (engineers, project managers, C-suite) want expertise
and industry-trend context; emphasize operational impact, ROI, and competitive positioning.
Editorial calendars in B2B often require outreach months in advance. [Channel V Media,
B2B vs B2C PR]

**B2C nuances:** broad consumer audiences care about lifestyle, entertainment, and
convenience angles. B2C press releases benefit from a narrative angle (a story, not
just a product) and immediate relevance to a lifestyle moment (seasonal, cultural,
trending topic). [Channel V Media, ibid.]

### Persuasion Principles

**Specificity as credibility:** Specific claims (named metrics, dollar amounts,
percentages) function as stronger credibility signals than generic statements.
A quote that states something specific and new (not a restatement of the lead)
serves as a persuasion device through authority and social proof. [Wylie Comm,
Press Release Quote Guide; Content Grip, ibid.]

**Social proof and third-party authority:** Quotes from customers or industry analysts
lend "additional perspectives and authenticity." This is a direct application of
Cialdini's social-proof principle — external validation outweighs internal claims.
[Content Grip, ibid.]

**Authority through attribution format:** Full name + title on first mention establishes
the authority/credibility of the speaker before their words are read — a structural
application of Cialdini's authority principle. ⚠ KNOWLEDGE-BASED synthesis connecting
the attribution-format finding to the authority principle.

**Overuse risk:** Avoid over-quoting. One well-chosen quote beats two mediocre ones.
If the lead + body text already cover the news, a quote that adds nothing gets removed
in editing anyway.

## Layer 3 — Goal and Distribution-Channel Variations

**Press releases sit largely outside the funnel-stage model** that applies to social
content. Their primary job is record-of-announcement and earned/owned media reach,
not funnel progression. ⚠ KNOWLEDGE-BASED characterization supported by the research
finding that no source frames a press release as a MOFU/BOFU nurture tool.

Instead, press releases in 2026 serve **three distinct functions simultaneously**.
Treat all three as legitimate goals a press release can serve:

### 1. Direct-to-journalist media pickup

**Goal:** Personalized outreach to journalists covering the relevant beat, with the
goal of earned media coverage.

**Calibration:**

- **Headline:** must be scannable and specific (journalists scanning 50–100 pitches
  a day read only the first line)
- **Lead paragraph:** must make the news clear in 1–2 sentences; assume the journalist
  will not read past the second paragraph
- **Length:** favor the 300–400 word range; shorter is better if content is complete
- **Quotes:** must be genuine insight, not generic enthusiasm (fluff gets cut first)
- **Why it still works:** personalization — identifying 20–50 journalists who cover
  the relevant beat and sending a personalized email with the release pasted directly
  in the body (not as an attachment) and referencing the journalist's recent work —
  is described as the single biggest factor in open rates. [Sharism.org, Press Release
  SEO 2026]

### 2. SEO / backlink and brand-entity signal via quality wire placement

**Goal:** Distribution through high-domain-authority wire services (PR Newswire,
Business Wire, GlobeNewswire, etc.) for backlinks, multi-site presence, and brand-entity
signals that both Google and AI systems reward.

**Calibration:**

- **Headline:** should contain the primary keyword naturally (not keyword-stuffed)
- **Quotable leads:** releases with distinct, extractable claims in the opening
  paragraph reward aggregator/AI-system pickup more than generic boilerplate language
- **Publisher quality matters more than distribution volume:** wire-service-distributed
  links require `rel="nofollow"` and do not pass PageRank directly. However,
  **strategic distribution through quality, high-domain-authority outlets still
  delivers measurable brand-entity signals and multi-site presence** that both Google
  and AI systems reward — SEO value now depends on publisher quality, not distribution
  volume. [Sharism.org, ibid.; EasyPRwire / RedPress, Press Release SEO Guide]
- **Cost reality:** $400–$1,500+ per release depending on targeting and wire service
  tier. [Sharism.org, ibid.]
- **Why it still works (but differently):** the era of blasting 500-word releases
  across low-quality wires for link juice is over, but the practice still works when
  paired with quality targeting and distinct claims. [EasyPRwire / RedPress, ibid.]

### 3. Owned record-of-announcement

**Goal:** A durable, company-controlled record of the announcement — published to
a company newsroom or website — even absent any third-party pickup.

**Calibration:**

- **Format and placement:** publish to a dedicated newsroom page with consistent
  navigation and archive capability so the release remains discoverable
- **Length:** can range wider (300–600 words) since it serves a reference/SEO
  function for future readers
- **Quotes and context:** can include internal perspective and company positioning
  that might feel promotional in a press pitch, because the audience is already
  internal or brand-aware
- **Why it matters:** even if journalists do not pick it up, the release becomes
  part of the company's public record and contributes to brand-entity signals over
  time. ⚠ KNOWLEDGE-BASED inference from the research finding that press releases
  serve this function.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] **Headline:** under 100 characters, title case, no ending punctuation (unless
      a question mark or exclamation point is grammatically necessary)
- [ ] **Dateline:** city in ALL CAPS, followed by long dash + space, then body text
      (`CITY — Body text…`)
- [ ] **Lead paragraph:** answers 5 Ws (who, what, when, where, why) in 50–75 words;
      uses specific, concrete language (not "excited," "thrilled," "innovative")
- [ ] **Total length:** 300–500 words (ideally ~400); under 600 words
- [ ] **AP style:** numerals 1–9 spelled out, 10+ as figures; percentages and money
      as digits; months 6+ letters abbreviated with dates; titles capitalized before
      names, lowercase after
- [ ] **Quotes:** 1–2 quotes maximum; each quote adds genuine insight or new information
      (not a restatement); multiple stakeholders represented if possible (customer,
      analyst, company)
- [ ] **Attribution:** full name + title on first mention, last name only on subsequent
      mentions
- [ ] **Boilerplate:** 50–100 words, third person, covers who the company is/helps,
      what it makes/does, where it's located
- [ ] **Media contact:** full name, title, phone, email at the end
- [ ] **Audience type, goal (pickup / SEO / owned record), and distribution channel
      confirmed with user**
- [ ] **Tone consistent with brand voice** (if loaded)
- [ ] **All claims in the release are either cited in the research files or marked
      ⚠ KNOWLEDGE-BASED** (release-specific, not brand-voice rules)
